Holidays in January

By: Amelie Pedersen

New Year

The new year is celebrated all around the world, but each civilization has measured time differently. Not everyone has the same new year. The United States uses a solar calendar, which means we celebrate on January 1. According to the lunar calendar, a year is 354 days instead of 365 days, so it will be celebrated on January 22 this year. In Mesopotamia around 4 millennia ago, they used the vernal equinox to choose the start of their new year; Which landed on March 20th. Egyptians, Persians, and Phoenicians

used the autumnal equinox, which resulted in the start of the new year around September 20th. Then the ancient Greeks picked the winter solstice, which made the new year December 20th.

Martin Luther King, Jr., Day

Martin Luther King, Jr., day is always celebrated on the third Monday of January. He was a Baptist minister who advocated the use of nonviolent means to end racial segregation. He drew national attention during a bus boycott. He founded the Southern Christian Leadership Conference in 1957 and led the 1963 March on Washington. He argued for the passage of the Civil Rights Act in 1964, which outlawed discrimination in public accommodations, facilities,and employment, and the Voting Rights Act of 1965. Martin Luther King, Jr., was awarded the Nobel Peace Prize in 1964 and was assassinated on April 4, 1968.

Presidents Day

Presidents Day is celebrated on the third Monday of February. It is thought of as the celebration of the birthdays and lives of all of the U.S. presidents. It started as a way to celebrate the first president of the United States. Because of this, it was called Washington's Birthday, but Congress debated whether to rename it Presidents’ Day to honor both George Washington and Abraham Lincon. Even though Congress rejected the name change, people began to call it Presidents Day. But it is still officially named Washington’s Birthday.

J-Term Summary

By: Aiden Cunningham

Would you like to go on a week-long trip down the Big Bend river, spending your nights camping on small river beds you just happened to come across using tents and supplies only from your boats? Well, you’re in luck, because every year the Post Oak School has something known as J-Term, which provides a wide range of experiences for Post Oak students to pursue their interests in a direct, hands-on way through real-life experiences. From cooking to economics to music to 3d design to multi-day canoeing and camping, and even custom single-person J-terms that can be whatever your passion is, you can truly pursue what you like to do.

So, what do all these J-Terms do? Let’s start with one of the most widely known ones and the one mentioned in the hook for this article, the Big Bend River Trip. During this J-term, students spend a week far away from home on the Rio Grande learning how to survive in the wild, paddling down the river and sleeping in tents out of their canoes. That’s just one of the J-terms however, there are many others that are equally fun and full of experiences. Personally, I took a J-term called “Art Of Cooking”, a two-week long term in which we worked with professional chefs to make a range of cultural dishes such as dumplings, sushi, and macarons.

 Other J-terms offer experiences such as learning the art of setting and mood music through video games, preparing

yourself for the world after high school with personal finance and stock market classes, exploring art passions by getting to transform a car into an art car, and much much more. Many schools offer art and creative advents as an option, but J-terms allow students to expand their passions and design an entire car with massive faux cakes on top and elegant paintwork on the side. J-term is a unique experience that allows students to pursue their interests in a hands-on way that provides a really great experience for students to participate in, and personally I think I’ve learned a lot from it.

Global Politics January 2023

By: Isa Pedersen

The main thing on many people’s minds at the moment, related to global politics, is the Ukraine-Russia war. If, somehow, you still do not know what is happening between Ukraine and Russia, in early February of 2022 Russia declared war on Ukraine because Putin, the leader of Russia, wanted Ukraine to be a part of Russia again. Ukraine is obviously against this, meaning they are fighting to keep their freedom. This has become a very gruesome war that has updates almost every single day. The latest update is about Germany and America sending tanks for Ukraine to use against Russia. Ukraine originally asked Germany to send them their tank, called the Leopard 2. Berlin did not say yes very quickly, and made a deal with Ukraine and America, saying that if America sent its tank, the M1 Abrams, then Germany would send the Leopard 2.

Both parties agreed to this deal, sending Ukraine their tanks. However, Ukraine will not be receiving these tanks very quickly. The tank from Germany will take months to arrive in Ukraine, and it will take the American tank much longer, closer to a year. Ukraine wanted those tanks to defeat a certain amount of Russian soldiers, however, these tanks will not be capable of that. With that being said, Germany and America sending Ukraine these tanks are inspiring other European countries to send heavy weaponry to Ukraine. The New York Times is calling this “a movement of heavy weaponry.” If all these countries send weaponry to Ukraine, it will give Ukraine a higher chance of defeating Russia.

In more local news, the Texas legislature is in session and there is one bill that is absolutely shocking to read and is just furthering the racism and discrimination towards the AAPI community. Senator Kolkhorst is trying to pass a bill that says citizens of China, North Korea, Iran, and Russia can no longer purchase real property in Texas. This includes houses. The bill would also ban companies from these countries to buy land for their companies. Gov. Abbot is backing this bill, taking a giant step backward in history, and harming millions of families all over Texas.
